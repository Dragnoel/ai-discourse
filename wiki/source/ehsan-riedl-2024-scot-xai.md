Opinion article in *Patterns* (CellPress, February 2024). Upol Ehsan and Mark O. Riedl at the Georgia Institute of Technology argue that forcing a single monolithic definition of explainable AI (XAI) is neither feasible nor desirable at this developmental stage. Drawing on the Social Construction of Technology (SCOT) framework, they propose that XAI's definitional plurality reflects productive interpretive flexibility among diverse stakeholder communities, and that premature closure would stifle innovation.

## Summary

Ehsan and Riedl open with the observation that researchers and developers in XAI are frustrated by the lack of consensus on what "explainability" means. The authors reframe this frustration: rather than treating definitional disagreement as a problem to be solved, they ask whether forcing premature closure might itself be harmful. Their analytical resource is SCOT, from which they draw three concepts — relevant social groups, interpretive flexibility, and closure — to understand the sociology of XAI.

Applying SCOT to XAI, the authors identify two primary relevant social groups (natural language processing and computer vision communities) whose different epistemic cultures generate distinct interpretations of explainability: NLP tends toward textual justification, CV toward saliency maps. These represent different "interpretive flexibilities" — legitimate, community-specific readings of what XAI must accomplish. Forcing standardisation to one community's interpretation would, by analogy to the evolution of the bicycle, prematurely close off alternatives before the technology has matured.

The practical upshot is a four-point argument: XAI is currently pluralistic; pluralism requires precision in communication even without definitional uniformity; SCOT provides analytical tools to understand who is making which claims, when, and why; and the field can make progress without monolithic consensus. The piece positions Human-Centered XAI (HCXAI) as a sociotechnical approach that takes seriously the plurality of stakeholder needs rather than imposing technical uniformity.

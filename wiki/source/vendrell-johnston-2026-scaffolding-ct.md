Peer-reviewed conceptual and normative article in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* (Elsevier, 2026). Mireia Vendrell (IIIA-CSIC, Spanish National Research Council, Barcelona) and Samantha-Kaye Johnston (Assessment and Evaluation Research Centre, University of Melbourne) synthesise research from cognitive psychology, educational theory, and AI ethics to develop a design-oriented pedagogical framework for integrating LLMs in higher education in ways that preserve and extend critical thinking.

## Summary

The article begins from the finding that unstructured LLM use poses three interconnected risks to student cognition: cognitive offloading (outsourcing effortful thought to AI, reducing the productive struggle essential for deep learning); metacognitive disengagement (students accepting AI outputs without reflection or verification, eroding self-monitoring capacity); and epistemic narrowing (AI systems optimised for convergence and fluency reducing exposure to ambiguous, contested, or counter-hegemonic knowledge). Vendrell and Johnston argue that these risks are not inherent to LLMs but are contingent on how they are integrated — pedagogical framing determines whether LLMs strengthen or substitute for critical thinking.

Drawing on Design-Based Research principles, the authors synthesise the theoretical and empirical literature into a normative pedagogical framework that identifies six essential epistemic processes underpinning critical engagement: conceptual interpretation, inferential reasoning, evaluative judgement, metacognitive regulation, intellectual curiosity, and epistemic integrity. Each process is defined, its vulnerability to unstructured LLM use is identified, and design responses are specified. The framework translates these into eight actionable design principles for LLM integration, including: preserving cognitive friction; positioning LLMs as provisional thinking partners rather than authoritative sources; embedding formative evaluation throughout learning; and sequencing AI-mediated tasks with AI-free phases to maintain independent epistemic capacity.

Two illustrative classroom scenarios are provided to demonstrate practical application. The framework is explicitly normative — it does not report empirical data but synthesises the evidence base to prescribe conditions for pedagogically sound LLM use. The authors position this as an intervention in the ongoing debate about whether to ban or embrace LLMs in HE, arguing that neither wholesale exclusion nor uncritical adoption serves educational goals; intentional, evidence-informed scaffolding is required.

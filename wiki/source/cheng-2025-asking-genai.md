Peer-reviewed empirical article in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* (Elsevier, 2025). Yixin Cheng and colleagues at Monash University (Australia) and Peking University (China) report the first empirical investigation of how seeking help from GenAI via question-asking affects academic performance on a writing task. The study finds that asking direct knowledge-seeking questions — rather than social coordination questions — mediates the performance advantage of the GenAI condition.

## Summary

Cheng et al. situate the study in the theoretical framework of help-seeking, arguing that a key affordance of GenAI is the capacity to seek help via unconstrained natural language question-asking. Prior research on help-seeking in face-to-face and online contexts has found positive associations between question-asking and learning outcomes, but these findings were developed in non-GenAI contexts. The study investigates whether seeking help via GenAI question-asking operates through the same mechanisms, and whether the kinds of questions matter.

Participants worked on a multi-source essay writing task in one of two experimental conditions: GenAI (using a GenAI-powered chatbot) or Tutor (a one-on-one human tutor accessible via chat interface). The study used quantitative ethnographic methods, coding all help-seeking episodes for question type (following a scheme based on Nelson-Le Gall's 1981 help-seeking taxonomy), and conducted mediation analysis using epistemic network analysis to examine whether question type mediated the relationship between condition and performance. The final essay quality was the primary performance outcome.

Participants in the GenAI condition asked significantly more questions overall than participants in the Tutor condition, and tended to ask one-off direct questions rather than engaging in extended conversational exchanges. The key finding is that asking direct, knowledge-seeking questions — driven by specific knowledge deficits rather than social coordination needs — mediated the performance advantage in the GenAI condition. The authors interpret this as evidence that GenAI can lower the social costs of admitting knowledge gaps, since students report being less worried about appearing incompetent to an AI than to a human tutor, and are consequently more willing to ask direct questions that positively affect performance.

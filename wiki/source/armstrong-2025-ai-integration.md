Doctoral dissertation (DBA in Information Technology, South College, Knoxville TN, 2025). Andrew Joseph Armstrong examines how public awareness of AI shapes human decision-making, emotional intelligence, and social behavior in everyday contexts. The study uses the Social Construction of Technology (SCOT) framework and draws on secondary data from the Pew Research Center's study of public AI awareness in everyday activities.

## Summary

Armstrong frames AI integration as the increasing use of AI technologies in daily life, and positions SCOT as the analytical lens for understanding how societal groups shape both the development and adoption of those technologies. The central research question asks how public awareness of AI influences decision-making, emotional intelligence, and social behavior — framing these as interdependent outcomes rather than separate effects.

The study applies thematic analysis to Pew Research Center survey data on public perceptions of and engagement with AI in everyday activities. Key findings highlight socioeconomic disparities, geographic differences, and generational variation in AI awareness and engagement. These disparities, Armstrong argues, constitute systemic barriers to equitable AI adoption, revealing patterns of unequal access that map onto pre-existing social inequalities rather than emerging from AI alone.

The practical implication Armstrong draws is that industries developing AI systems should prioritize transparent communication, ethical practices, and targeted outreach to foster trust and inclusivity across diverse populations. The dissertation's theoretical contribution lies in applying SCOT to AI integration at the societal level, making the case that AI's effects on social behavior are not technologically determined but are shaped by the social contexts in which AI is encountered and understood.

Research note in *MIS Quarterly* (December 2024). Ekaterina Jussupow (TU Darmstadt), Izak Benbasat (University of British Columbia), and Armin Heinzl (University of Mannheim) provide an integrative conceptual analysis of algorithm aversion and appreciation in decision-making. The paper argues that the empirical literature on these constructs is fragmented due to inconsistent definitions and incompatible measurement approaches, and proposes a classification scheme to guide future research.

## Abstract

> People have conflicting responses for support from algorithms or humans in decision-making. On the one hand, they fail to benefit from algorithms due to algorithm aversion, as they reject decisions provided by algorithms more frequently than those made by humans. On the other hand, many prefer algorithmic over human advice, an effect of algorithm appreciation. However, currently, we lack a shared understanding of these constructs' meaning and measurements, resulting in a lack of theoretical integration of empirical findings. Thus, in this research note, we conceptualize algorithm aversion as the preference for humans over algorithms in decision-making and analyze approaches in current research to measure this preference. First, we outline the implications of focusing on a specific understanding of algorithms as computational procedures or as embedded in material or nonmaterial objects. Then, we classify four decision configurations that distinguish individuals' evaluations of algorithms, human advisors, their own judgments, or combinations of these. Consequently, we develop a classification scheme that provides guidance for future research to develop more specific hypotheses on the direction of preferences (aversion vs. appreciation) and the effect of moderators.

**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.25300/MISQ/2024/18512

## Summary

The algorithm aversion literature has accumulated a body of conflicting empirical findings, with some studies finding that people distrust algorithms and others finding that they prefer them. Jussupow et al. argue that this conflict is partly artefactual — the result of researchers using different definitions of "algorithm," different understandings of "aversion," and incompatible decision configurations — rather than reflecting genuine population-level inconsistency. Their aim is to provide conceptual scaffolding for cumulative knowledge-building.

The paper makes two primary contributions. First, it specifies algorithm aversion as the preference for humans over algorithms in decision-making, adopting a deliberately general definition that encompasses both AI chatbots and statistical formulas. It distinguishes two perspectives on algorithms: as computational procedures (the traditional understanding dating to Meehl 1954 and Dietvorst et al. 2015) and as IT artifacts embedded in nonmaterial or material objects (including software programs, robots, and autonomous vehicles). Second, it classifies four decision configurations distinguishing whether individuals evaluate algorithms, human advisors, their own judgments, or combinations of these, and shows how different configurations measure different aspects of the preference construct. A classification scheme derived from these axes provides guidance for designing studies with commensurable results.

The authors identify the inaugural use of "algorithm aversion" as Dietvorst et al. (2015), who defined it as people's tendency to choose human forecasters over statistical algorithms even when algorithms are demonstrably more accurate. Jussupow et al. argue that much subsequent research has drifted from this definition, creating measurement heterogeneity that obscures theoretical progress. Their integrative scheme is positioned as a resource for resolving this fragmentation.

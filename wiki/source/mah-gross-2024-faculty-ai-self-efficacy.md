Peer-reviewed empirical article in the *International Journal of Educational Technology in Higher Education* (Springer, 2024). Dana-Kristin Mah and Nele Groß at Leuphana University of Lüneburg report a quantitative survey study of N=122 faculty members examining AI self-efficacy, perceived benefits and challenges, AI use, and professional development needs. Latent class analysis yields four distinct faculty profiles.

## Summary

The study is motivated by the underdevelopment of faculty perspectives in AI-in-education research, which has concentrated disproportionately on student experience. Mah and Groß argue that faculty are uniquely positioned as both users and role models for appropriate AI use, and that their preparedness, self-efficacy, and professional development needs must be understood to enable meaningful AI integration into teaching.

A structured questionnaire was administered to 122 faculty members. Measures included Bandura-based AI self-efficacy items, perceived benefits and challenges of AI in teaching, AI tool use frequency and type, and professional development interests. Latent class analysis (LCA) was performed on these measures to identify distinct faculty profiles. Four profiles emerged: optimistic (high self-efficacy, high use, positive benefit perceptions); critical (moderate self-efficacy, active use, significant concern about risks); critically reflected (high self-efficacy but cautious adoption, weighing benefits against risks); and neutral (low engagement and self-efficacy, limited concern).

The key finding is that the relationship between AI self-efficacy and actual usage is moderated by profile membership: it is significant for optimistic faculty but not for other profiles. The most commonly identified benefit was greater equity in education; the most commonly identified challenges were student and faculty AI literacy gaps, with the majority of respondents expressing interest in professional development. The implication is that one-size-fits-all AI professional development is unlikely to be effective; support services must be tailored to profile-specific needs, especially given that the largest profile by implication includes critically reflective faculty who need evidence and ethical scaffolding rather than enthusiasm.

Peer-reviewed empirical article in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* (Elsevier, 2026). Ruonan Wang, Yan Yin (Hubei University), and Yongbo Cao (Wuhan Huaxia Institute of Technology) propose a LLM-based pedagogical framework integrating active learning, inquiry-based learning (IBL), and adaptive learning principles for L2 writing instruction, and validate it through a quasi-experimental study of N=50 sophomores.

## Summary

Second language (L2) writing instruction presents persistent challenges: providing personalised, process-oriented feedback at scale; sustaining student engagement; and adapting instruction to individual needs. Wang et al. argue that while GenAI tools like ChatGPT offer potential solutions, their application in L2 writing lacks a robust pedagogical conceptualisation — most integration attempts have been fragmentary, grounded in a single pedagogical approach rather than a theoretically coherent multi-principle framework.

The proposed framework synthesises three well-established learning models. Active learning positions students as participants in knowledge construction rather than passive recipients; students must do things and reflect on what they do. Inquiry-based learning (IBL) frames knowledge creation around student-centred inquiry and investigation, with teachers in a facilitative rather than directive role. Adaptive learning uses technology to dynamically adjust content and instruction to individual learner profiles. The framework integrates ChatGPT as a collaborative partner within a six-step writing instruction process: perceive the topic, understand the aim, transfer the frame, analyse the skills, synthesise the argumentation, and output the writing — with teacher, AI, and learner in a tripartite dynamic.

Validation through a quasi-experimental case study with 50 sophomore L2 writing students found that the framework significantly enhanced writing outcomes and motivation compared to baseline. The study contributes a coherent pedagogical rationale for ChatGPT integration in L2 writing, arguing that the technology's benefits are contingent on how instruction is structured rather than on the capabilities of the tool alone.

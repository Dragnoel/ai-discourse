Peer-reviewed empirical article in the *Journal of Behavioral Decision Making* (Wiley, 2023). Mark Himmelstein and David Budescu at Fordham University report two studies on forecasting advice-taking in geopolitical and economic contexts, funded by the Intelligence Advanced Research Projects Activity. The central finding — that stated preferences for human versus algorithmic advice do not predict actual advice use — challenges a foundational assumption in algorithm aversion research.

## Abstract

> Past research has found that people treat advice differently depending on its source. In many cases, people seem to prefer human advice to algorithms, but in others, there is a reversal, and people seem to prefer algorithmic advice. Across two studies, we examine the persuasiveness of, and judges' preferences for, advice from different sources when forecasting geopolitical events. We find that judges report domain-specific preferences, preferring human advice in the domain of politics and algorithmic advice in the domain of economics. In Study 2, participants report a preference for hybrid advice, that combines human and algorithmic sources, to either one on it's own regardless of domain. More importantly, we find that these preferences did not affect persuasiveness of advice from these different sources, regardless of domain. Judges were primarily sensitive to quantitative features pertaining to the similarity between their initial beliefs and the advice they were offered, such as the distance between them and the relative advisor confidence, when deciding whether to revise their initial beliefs in light of advice, rather than the source that generated the advice.

**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1002/bdm.2285

## Summary

Prior research on algorithm aversion has concentrated on eliciting participants' preferences for human versus algorithmic advisors and treating those preferences as predictive of advice uptake. Himmelstein and Budescu identify a largely overlooked validity problem: verbal statements of preference and actual belief revision are dissociable constructs, and the literature has frequently conflated them. Their studies examine geopolitical and economic forecasting contexts — specifically drawing on Hybrid Forecasting Competition data — where forecasters decide whether to revise their probability estimates in light of algorithmic or human advice.

In Study 1, judges forecasting political and economic events showed domain-specific stated preferences: human advice was preferred in the political domain, algorithmic advice in the economics domain. However, these preferences did not significantly predict persuasiveness (measured by Weight on Advice) regardless of domain. Judges were primarily sensitive to the quantitative relationship between their initial beliefs and the advice — specifically, the distance between their estimate and the advice, and the advisor's reported confidence — rather than to the source (human vs. algorithmic) of the advice. Study 2 replicated the preference-use disconnection and found that a hybrid condition (combining human and algorithmic sources) was preferred over either alone, without producing meaningfully different persuasiveness.

These results introduce a critical constraint on the algorithm aversion/appreciation literature: studies that measure preferences alone cannot draw conclusions about actual use. The authors note that the findings also reframe the "buzzword" effect (whether labeling advice as "AI," "deep learning," or "neural network" makes it more or less trusted) as primarily a preference phenomenon, not a behavior change.

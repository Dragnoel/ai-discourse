Peer-reviewed empirical article in the *Journal of Marketing* (SAGE/AMA, 2025 volume, submitted 2024). Stephanie Tully (USC Marshall School), Chiara Longoni (Bocconi University), and Gil Appel (GWU) report ten studies — four surveys, cross-country data, and six additional studies — demonstrating a counter-intuitive negative relationship between objective AI literacy and AI receptivity, mediated by perception of AI as magical.

## Summary

The prevailing assumption in AI adoption research and marketing practice is that greater AI literacy leads to greater receptivity: better understanding should produce more positive attitudes and more willingness to use AI products. Tully et al. directly challenge this assumption through a multi-study program of research. They find that consumers with lower objective AI literacy exhibit greater AI receptivity — a relationship they term the "lower literacy-higher receptivity link." Importantly, this link is not explained by differences in perceptions of AI's capability, ethicality, or feared impact on humanity.

The mechanism Tully et al. identify is perceptual: people with lower AI literacy are more likely to perceive AI systems as magical, and to experience feelings of awe when AI performs tasks they associate with distinctively human attributes (such as having a personality, feeling emotions, or holding beliefs). AI systems executing such tasks appear to transcend the boundaries of conventional technology for lower-literacy observers. In contrast, consumers with higher AI literacy recognise that AI does not actually possess human attributes when executing these tasks, thereby reducing perceptions of magicality and dampening awe — and with it, receptivity. The literacy-receptivity link is moderated by task type: it attenuates for tasks not associated with distinctively human attributes.

The practical implication is counterintuitive for marketers: AI literacy education, typically advocated as a route to informed adoption, may inadvertently reduce the appeal of AI products by demystifying them. The authors suggest that efforts to "demystify AI" may reduce receptivity among currently receptive lower-literacy consumers while not necessarily converting higher-literacy skeptics.

Peer-reviewed empirical article in *Teaching and Teacher Education* (Elsevier, 2024). Yanzhen Lan, affiliated with Qingdao University of Science and Technology (China) and the University of Cambridge (UK), reports a mixed-methods case study of N=216 survey participants and 15 in-depth qualitative interviews from a Chinese university-level AI-enhanced teacher training programme. The study uses the IBM-SDT framework (Identity-Based Motivation combined with Self-Determination Theory) to examine how teacher professional identity (TPI) tensions shape motivations to integrate AI.

## Summary

The study begins from the observation that rapid AI integration into education places teachers in a position of identity renegotiation: they must balance traditional pedagogical beliefs and established professional identities with the demands and possibilities introduced by AI-enhanced methods. Existing research on TPI in AI contexts has been largely theoretical; Lan's study provides empirical grounding from a Chinese multidisciplinary university setting, with attention to how TPI tensions manifest at the institutional level.

The IBM-SDT framework integrates two complementary theoretical lenses. Identity-Based Motivation (IBM, Oyserman 2015) posits that motivation and behavior are influenced by the dynamic construction of identities, the relevance of those identities to current contexts, and the interpretation of difficulties encountered in identity-congruent actions. Self-Determination Theory (SDT, Deci & Ryan) highlights the role of intrinsic versus extrinsic motivation. Together, they allow the study to examine how TPI tensions — manifested as pull-and-push forces between competing identities — generate specific motivation types and ultimately influence AI integration behavior.

Surveys of 216 attendees and qualitative interviews with 15 selected teachers identified three conceptual TPI tension dimensions: groupness versus individuality (collective vs. individual identity orientation), humanity versus technology (humanistic teaching values vs. technological demands), and continuity versus openness (stable identity vs. openness to change). From these tensions, three AI role models emerged: the Navigator (using AI to guide learning paths), the Collaborator (using AI as a co-teaching partner), and the Inventor (innovating with AI to develop new pedagogical approaches). The study underscores that tailored AI-enhanced teacher training in China must harmonise educators' diverse identities and motivation types.

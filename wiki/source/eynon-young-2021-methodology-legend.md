Peer-reviewed empirical article in *Science, Technology, & Human Values* (SAGE, 2021). Rebecca Eynon and Erin Young at the University of Oxford report a SCOT-informed analysis of 34 in-depth interviews with stakeholders from academia, commerce, and policy engaged with AI for lifelong learning. The study identifies three distinct technological frames for AI and argues that the commercial sector's frame wields the most influence on AI design, crowding out educational concerns.

## Summary

The study is motivated by the observation that AI for lifelong learning sits at the intersection of two major contemporary concerns: the fourth industrial revolution's demand for continuous workforce reskilling, and the rapid advancement of AI as a tool for delivering learning at scale. Eynon and Young note that stakeholders disagree substantially about what AI for lifelong learning is for, and use SCOT — specifically the concept of "technological frames" — to characterise these disagreements.

Thirty-four in-depth interviews were conducted with stakeholders from academia, commerce, and policy organisations engaged with AI in lifelong learning contexts, supplemented by document analysis. SCOT analysis identified three distinct technological frames, one per stakeholder group. Academia tends to frame AI instrumentally, through a lens of methodological rigour: AI as a tool for generating insights and improving understanding of learning processes. Industry (the commercial sector) frames AI as a delivery mechanism for scalable, personalised learning experiences — optimising for efficiency, retention, and measurable outcomes. Policy groups frame AI as a vehicle for "reskilling society" for an AI future, often adopting uncritical optimism about AI's transformative potential.

The key analytical claim is that commercial sector frames wield disproportionate influence because commercial actors possess the most design power — they build the systems — while academic and policy frames are more reactive. Eynon and Young argue that without greater interaction among stakeholder groups and a clearer articulation of what educational outcomes AI should serve, AI for lifelong learning risks serving commercial efficiency at the expense of democratic education goals.

Peer-reviewed empirical article in *Computers & Education* (Elsevier, 2025). Fangzhou Jin and colleagues at the University of Hong Kong report a metaphor analysis study of N=277 international postgraduate students from 14 countries studying at 18 UK universities. The study identifies 53 unique metaphors for GenAI in academic writing and organises them into a four-category taxonomy called the 4T Pyramid Model.

## Summary

The study addresses two gaps in the literature: most research on GenAI in academic writing has used positivist experimental methods and drawn on single cultural backgrounds, producing limited insight into the diverse, discipline-specific perceptions that international students — who face unique academic writing challenges — bring to GenAI tools. Jin et al. argue that metaphor analysis can reveal the full range of positive and negative perceptions, including affective and implicit dimensions that surveys and experiments do not capture.

Data collection proceeded in two phases. First, 277 international postgraduate students at 18 UK universities completed a demographic and metaphor-elicitation survey. Second, a subsample provided screenshots of their actual GenAI interactions, enabling validation of the metaphors against real use patterns. Qualitative and quantitative analysis of the 53 metaphors yielded four conceptual categories, grouped into what the authors call the 4T Pyramid Model: Technical Support (exemplary metaphor: high-heeled shoes — functional assistance without which certain tasks become difficult); Text Development (compass — guiding the writing process toward clarity and direction); Transformative Potential (Spider-Man — ability to produce effects beyond ordinary human capacity); and Threat (drug — dependency, loss of original voice, ethical risk).

Most students, across disciplines, conceptualised GenAI as primarily Transformative rather than merely a writing tool. Disciplinary background significantly influenced metaphor choice: students in social sciences and humanities showed more ambivalence and were more likely to invoke threat metaphors, while students in STEM and business disciplines showed stronger Technical Support framings. The study contributes a cross-disciplinary, cross-cultural empirical basis for designing AI literacy education that addresses the threat dimension alongside the transformative.

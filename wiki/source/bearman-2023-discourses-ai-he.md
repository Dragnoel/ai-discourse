Peer-reviewed critical literature review in *Higher Education* (Springer, 2023). Margaret Bearman, Juliana Ryan, and Rola Ajjawi conducted a systematic discourse analysis of 29 peer-reviewed articles from the top ten higher education journals that used the term "artificial intelligence." The study identifies two dominant Discourses — in the sense of Gee's discourse theory — through which AI is constructed in this literature, and calls for research that attends to the social and ethical implications of AI-mediated higher education.

## Summary

Bearman and colleagues begin from the observation that AI holds enormous implications for higher education, yet references to AI in the relevant research literature are frequently vague, contested, and poorly defined. The study's aim is not to assess what AI can do in HE, but to understand how AI is discursively constructed by the researchers who study it — and thus what assumptions, values, and power relations are inscribed in the literature's framing of the technology.

The review searched ten leading higher education journals (including *Assessment & Evaluation in Higher Education*, *Teaching in Higher Education*, and *Internet and Higher Education*) for any article using the term "artificial intelligence" up to November 2020, yielding 29 articles after exclusion. Bearman et al. employed Gee's (2004) discourse analytic approach, analysing both explicit definitions and implicit associations in each text. The analysis identified two prominent Discourses. The first, the [[Discourse of Imperative Change]], constructs AI as an inevitable force to which all must respond, framing adaptation as urgent and non-optional. The second, the [[Discourse of Altering Authority]], constructs AI as decentring the teacher, redistributing authority across students, machines, and institutions.

A central finding is that only 3 of 146 papers in Zawacki-Richter et al.'s (2019) wider review considered ethical implications, a "stunningly low" proportion. Bearman et al. argue that by constructing AI primarily as a technological innovation with technical rather than social implications, the HE literature limits its own capacity to grapple with questions of accountability, labour, and equity in AI-mediated education. The review prompts a call for new research foci that trace accountability in AI-mediated practices and investigate how AI influences learning and teaching relationships.

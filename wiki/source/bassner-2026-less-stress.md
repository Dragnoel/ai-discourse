Peer-reviewed article in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* (Elsevier, 2026). Patrick Bassner and colleagues at the Technical University of Munich report a three-arm randomised controlled trial examining how different AI support types affect performance, learning, cognitive load, and motivation in an introductory programming course. The study finds that AI tools improve exercise scores without producing corresponding learning gains — a pattern the authors call the "comfort trap."

## Abstract

> **Introduction:** Generative AI is reshaping programming education, yet its effects on conceptual learning, intrinsic motivation, and cognitive load remain unclear. This study tests whether assistance deepens understanding or primarily boosts task completion, and how scaffolded versus answer-giving designs matter.
>
> **Objectives:** This study compares performance, learning, cognitive load, frustration, and motivation across three AI support types, and examines students' perceptions.
>
> **Methods:** A three-arm randomized controlled trial was conducted in an introductory programming (CS1) course at TUM (N = 275). Participants completed a 90-minute exercise on concurrency, implementing a parallel sum with threading in one of three conditions: (1) *Iris*, a scaffolded tutor providing calibrated hints while withholding full solutions; (2) *ChatGPT*, unrestricted assistance that can provide complete solutions; (3) no-AI control using traditional web resources. Pre- and post-knowledge tests and a code comprehension task measured learning, while auto-graded test coverage measured performance. Validated scales captured intrinsic, germane, and extraneous cognitive load, frustration, and intrinsic motivation.
>
> **Results:** Both AI groups achieved substantially higher exercise scores than the control group, with distinct distributions: ChatGPT users clustered at high scores, control participants at low scores, and Iris users spread across the full range. Despite these performance gains, neither AI condition produced greater pre–post knowledge gains or code-comprehension advantages. Both AI groups reported lower frustration and reduced extraneous and germane load than the control group, while intrinsic load did not differ. Only Iris increased intrinsic motivation. Students rated ChatGPT as easier to use and more helpful.
>
> **Conclusion:** In this setting, generative AI acted primarily as a performance aid rather than a learning enhancer. Scaffolded, hint-first design preserved motivational benefits, whereas AI providing unrestricted solutions encouraged a "comfort trap" where students' preferences misaligned with pedagogical effectiveness. These findings motivate scaffolded AI integration and assessment designs resilient to environments where performance no longer reliably tracks understanding.

**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.caeai.2025.100537

## Summary

The study addresses a tension in programming education discourse: critics argue that AI tools encourage cognitive outsourcing and dependency, while proponents claim they can scaffold novices through difficult syntax and provide expert-level feedback. The study was designed to test whether AI assistance deepens understanding or primarily boosts task completion, and whether design choices — specifically whether AI provides full solutions or calibrated hints — determine educational impact.

A three-arm RCT enrolled N=275 students in an introductory programming course at TU Munich. Participants were assigned to one of three conditions: Iris, a scaffolded tutor providing calibrated hints while withholding full solutions; ChatGPT, providing unrestricted assistance including complete code on demand; and a no-AI control group using conventional web resources. The primary outcomes were exercise performance scores and pre-to-post knowledge gains; secondary outcomes included cognitive load (intrinsic, germane, and extraneous subscales), frustration, and intrinsic motivation.

Both AI conditions achieved substantially higher exercise scores than the control group. However, neither AI condition produced statistically significant pre-to-post learning gains or code-comprehension improvements relative to the control. Both AI groups reported lower frustration and reduced extraneous and germane cognitive load, but these reductions did not convert into learning advantages. Only the Iris group showed increased intrinsic motivation; the ChatGPT group showed no motivational benefit despite being rated as more helpful and easier to use. The authors conclude that unrestricted AI assistance created a "comfort trap," where students' performance preferences misaligned with pedagogical effectiveness, and call for scaffolded AI integration and assessment designs resilient to environments where performance no longer reliably tracks understanding.

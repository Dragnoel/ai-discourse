Systematic literature review published in the *Australasian Journal of Educational Technology* (ASCILITE, 2024). Sasha Nikolic and colleagues from the University of Wollongong and partner institutions conducted a review of teaching academics' attitudes, intentions, and behaviors toward AI and GenAI in higher education, using the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) as the organising framework.

## Abstract

> The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) has outpaced existing research and regulatory frameworks in higher education, leading to varied institutional responses. Although some educators and institutions have embraced AI and generative AI (GenAI), other individuals remain cautious. This systematic literature review explored teaching academics' attitudes, perceptions and intentions towards AI and GenAI, identifying perceived benefits and obstacles. Utilising the unified theory of acceptance and use of technology framework, this study reveals positive attitudes towards AI's efficiency and teaching enhancement, but also significant concerns about academic integrity, accuracy, reliability, skill development and the need for comprehensive training and policies. These findings underscore the necessity for institutional support to navigate the integration of AI and GenAI in tertiary education.

## Summary

The review was motivated by the rapid pace of GenAI adoption in higher education and the underdevelopment of research on faculty — as distinct from students — as technology users and decision-makers. Nikolic et al. note that while student GenAI use has attracted substantial research attention, faculty attitudes and adoption determinants remain incompletely understood, making it difficult to develop evidence-based institutional policy and support frameworks. The review addresses four research questions: general faculty attitudes and perceptions toward AI/GenAI; perceived benefits and obstacles; determinants of adoption or intention to adopt; and how these factors interact according to UTAUT constructs.

The systematic review draws on global literature and synthesises findings through UTAUT's four core constructs: performance expectancy (perceived usefulness), effort expectancy (perceived ease of use), social influence (peer and institutional pressure), and facilitating conditions (organisational and technical support). The review finds that faculty generally express positive attitudes toward AI's potential to enhance teaching efficiency and student engagement, but significant concerns cluster around academic integrity, accuracy and reliability of AI outputs, skill development implications for students, and the need for comprehensive training and institutional policies.

Institutional support and training availability emerge as critical moderators: in their absence, faculty tend toward caution or resistance even when they hold generally positive attitudes. The review identifies a consistent gap between positive attitudes in principle and actual adoption behaviors, attributing this gap to inadequate facilitating conditions rather than to principled opposition. The implication is that institutional investment in training, clear policy guidance, and ongoing support is necessary to convert positive attitudes into pedagogically productive adoption.

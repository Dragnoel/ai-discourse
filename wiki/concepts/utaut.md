# UTAUT

The Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) is a technology adoption framework developed by Venkatesh et al. (2003) that synthesises eight prior acceptance models including TAM (Technology Acceptance Model), TPB (Theory of Planned Behavior), and others. UTAUT identifies four core constructs as predictors of behavioral intention to use a technology and subsequent actual usage.

## Core constructs

- **Performance expectancy**: the degree to which individuals believe using the technology will provide benefits in performing certain activities (similar to perceived usefulness in TAM)
- **Effort expectancy**: the degree of ease associated with using the technology (similar to perceived ease of use in TAM)
- **Social influence**: the degree to which individuals perceive that important others believe they should use the new system
- **Facilitating conditions**: the degree to which individuals believe that organisational and technical infrastructure exists to support system use

Gender, age, experience, and voluntariness of use are specified as moderating variables.

## Use in AI discourse

UTAUT has become the dominant framework for studying AI adoption in higher education. In this collection it is applied in three papers: Nikolic et al. (2024) use UTAUT to organise a systematic literature review of faculty AI attitudes; Xu and Xiong (2026) apply an extended UTAUT (adding emotions, AI literacy, AI self-efficacy) to Chinese FL student GenAI acceptance; and Verano-Tacoronte et al. (2025) use two UTAUT constructs (effort expectancy and performance expectancy) as mediators between faculty anxiety and adoption intention.

A recurring limitation noted across these studies is that the base UTAUT model does not incorporate affective or identity dimensions, which may be particularly important when the technology in question (GenAI) has distinctive human-like qualities and raises questions about professional role and epistemic authority.

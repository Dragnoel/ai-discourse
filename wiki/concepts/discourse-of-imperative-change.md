# Discourse of Imperative Change

The Discourse of Imperative Change is one of two dominant Discourses identified by Bearman et al. (2023) in their critical discourse analysis of 29 peer-reviewed articles from the top higher education journals that reference artificial intelligence. In this Discourse, AI is constructed as an inevitable force that is transforming every domain of social life, and to which all actors — students, faculty, institutions — must adapt as a matter of urgency. Adaptation is framed as non-optional.

## Characteristics

The Discourse of Imperative Change positions AI as an external force with a pre-determined trajectory. Institutional actors are cast primarily as responders rather than shapers; the appropriate stance is described as preparation, readiness, and adaptation. The logic is teleological: AI will transform higher education, and institutions should act now to position themselves for that transformation. Critique of or resistance to AI integration tends to be marginalised or absent.

Bearman et al. identify this Discourse as pervasive in the HE AI literature to the point of foreclosing the kind of critical, social analysis that might interrogate the assumptions built into AI development itself. The Discourse tends to privilege technical and efficiency framings of AI over social and ethical ones.

## Relation to adjacent concepts

The Discourse of Imperative Change is complementary to — and often co-occurring with — the [[Discourse of Altering Authority]], Bearman et al.'s second identified Discourse. Where imperative change focuses on the inevitability of AI adoption, altering authority focuses on its effect on educational relationships and power. Together, they constitute the dominant discursive frame within which AI in HE is discussed in the 2020 and prior literature.

# Social Construction of Technology

Social Construction of Technology (SCOT) is a theoretical framework in science and technology studies, developed by Wiebe Bijker and Trevor Pinch (1984), arguing that the meaning, design, and adoption of technologies are not technologically determined but are shaped by the social groups that interact with them. The central analytical concepts are relevant social groups, interpretive flexibility, and closure.

## Core concepts

**Relevant social groups** are stakeholders with a stake in a technology — users, developers, regulators, affected communities — each of whom has their own interpretation of what the technology is and what it should do.

**Interpretive flexibility** describes the condition in which a technology has different meanings for different social groups: the same artifact can be understood as serving different purposes, having different risks, or requiring different design choices depending on which group is doing the interpreting.

**Closure** is the process by which interpretive flexibility stabilises: over time, certain interpretations win out, the technology's form becomes relatively fixed, and the earlier openness to radical alternatives diminishes.

## Use in AI discourse

SCOT has been applied in this collection by three papers addressing distinct objects. Armstrong (2025) applies SCOT at the societal level to examine how public awareness of AI integration is shaped by social group membership. Eynon and Young (2021) use SCOT's technological frames concept to show how academia, industry, and policy stakeholders construct AI for lifelong learning through incompatible framings. Ehsan and Riedl (2024) apply SCOT to explainable AI, arguing that XAI's definitional pluralism reflects productive interpretive flexibility and that premature closure would harm innovation.

# Performance-Learning Dissociation

Performance-learning dissociation refers to the empirical finding, documented by Bassner et al. (2026), that AI assistance can produce large improvements in task performance — exercise scores, output quality — without producing corresponding gains in underlying conceptual knowledge or skill acquisition. The phrase captures the decoupling of observable performance indicators from durable learning outcomes.

## Significance

The dissociation matters because task performance is often used as a proxy for learning in educational assessment. When AI tools are available, high performance can be achieved without the cognitive engagement necessary for schema construction, skill development, or long-term retention. Bassner et al. demonstrate this in a controlled experiment: both ChatGPT and Iris conditions achieved substantially higher exercise scores than the control group, but neither showed significant pre-to-post knowledge gains. The control group, despite performing more poorly on exercises, demonstrated equivalent conceptual learning.

Bassner et al. relate this finding to the concept of the [[Assistance Dilemma]] and to the notion of "desirable difficulties" — the idea that temporary performance impairment during acquisition can enhance long-term retention and transfer (Bjork 1994). AI tools, by eliminating difficulties that are desirable rather than merely frustrating, may create an "illusion of competence."

## Counter-claims

Yuxian (2025) finds a synergistic effect between LLM assistance and critical thinking guidance on both declarative and procedural learning — suggesting that performance-learning dissociation is not an inherent property of AI-assisted learning but a design artifact. When AI is combined with appropriate pedagogical scaffolding, both performance and learning can improve. This positions the dissociation as a consequence of pedagogical design choices rather than of AI itself.

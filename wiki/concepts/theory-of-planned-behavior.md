# Theory of Planned Behavior

The Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB), developed by Icek Ajzen (1991) building on the Theory of Reasoned Action, is a social psychological model predicting individual behavioral intentions and behaviors from three antecedents: attitude toward the behavior (positive or negative evaluation of performing the behavior), subjective norms (perceived social pressure from important others to perform or avoid the behavior), and perceived behavioral control (perceived ease or difficulty of performing the behavior, reflecting both self-efficacy and controllability).

## Modified TPB with moral obligation

In the context of academic integrity and AI use, Huang et al. (2025) extend the TPB with a fourth construct: moral obligation — the sense of moral duty or ethical responsibility that may influence intentions to engage in or avoid dishonest behavior, independent of attitudes, norms, and control beliefs. This extension reflects the observation that GenAI-related academic misconduct occupies ethical gray areas that the standard TPB does not fully address: students may engage in behaviors they consider partially dishonest despite positive normative pressure, or avoid behaviors they consider acceptable due to moral discomfort.

## Related frameworks

UTAUT incorporates several TPB constructs (subjective norms maps onto social influence; perceived behavioral control onto effort expectancy and facilitating conditions). The distinction is that UTAUT was designed specifically for technology adoption contexts while TPB is a general behavioral prediction model applied across domains.

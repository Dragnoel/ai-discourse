# Discourse of Altering Authority

The Discourse of Altering Authority is the second of two dominant Discourses identified by Bearman et al. (2023) in their discourse analysis of higher education AI literature. In this Discourse, AI is constructed as decentring the teacher and spreading authority across a wider network of actors: students, machines, commercial providers, and institutional systems. The teacher's traditional role as primary knowledge authority is described as changing, diminishing, or being redistributed.

## Characteristics

The Discourse of Altering Authority does not necessarily position AI as a threat to teachers; it may frame the redistribution of authority neutrally or even positively, as a shift toward more student-centred or technology-supported learning. However, Bearman et al. note that this framing systematically obscures questions of accountability: when authority is distributed across teachers, AI systems, learning management software, and commercial vendors, the question of who is responsible for educational outcomes becomes unclear.

This Discourse intersects with — and may partially produce — questions about teacher professional identity. If AI redistributes authority, teachers must renegotiate their sense of professional role. This connection links the Discourse of Altering Authority to empirical findings in Lan (2024), where teacher professional identity tensions emerge directly from AI integration in teacher training.

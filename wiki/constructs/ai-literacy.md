# AI Literacy

AI literacy is the degree to which an individual understands what AI systems are, how they function, and what their capabilities and limitations entail. It is increasingly treated as a component of digital literacy, though distinct from general technology competence due to AI's probabilistic, opaque, and often anthropomorphised character.

## Definitional tension

No consensus definition exists. Some researchers treat AI literacy as objective knowledge (factual understanding of how systems work); others treat it as self-assessed competence or confidence. This distinction is not merely methodological: Tully et al. (2024) find that people with lower objective AI literacy are more receptive to AI — because they perceive AI as magical and experience awe at its outputs — whereas Xu and Xiong (2026) find that self-reported AI literacy predicts greater adoption intention. These findings are consistent if the constructs are measuring different things, but the discourse conflates them.

## Cross-source notes

A potential contradiction between Tully et al. (2024) and Xu and Xiong (2026) is flagged in `wiki/_review.md`. Claim pages derived from these sources should be careful to specify whether the literacy measure is objective or self-reported and whether the outcome is receptivity, adoption intention, or actual usage.

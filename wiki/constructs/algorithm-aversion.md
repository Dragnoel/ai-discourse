# Algorithm Aversion

Algorithm aversion is the preference for human judgment over algorithmic judgment in decision-making. It is considered a significant obstacle to the beneficial use of algorithms in professional and everyday decision contexts, since algorithms routinely outperform unaided human judgment in many domains.

## Definition and scope

The construct was first named by Dietvorst et al. (2015), who showed that people punish algorithms for errors more harshly than they punish equivalent human errors, leading to rejection of algorithmic advice even when that advice is demonstrably superior. Jussupow et al. (2024) later reconceptualised algorithm aversion as the preference for humans over algorithms in decision-making, distinguishing it from algorithm appreciation and classifying it across four decision configurations.

## Operationalisation

Algorithm aversion is operationalised primarily via the JAS paradigm. Participants make an initial estimate, receive advice labeled as either human or algorithmic, and make a final estimate. WOA captures the degree of revision. The gap in WOA between algorithmic and human advice conditions indexes the direction and degree of aversion or appreciation.

## Contested findings

Three sources in this collection engage the same empirical terrain but reach different conclusions. See [[Algorithm Appreciation]] and [[Disputed Claims]] for the aversion–appreciation tension. Himmelstein and Budescu (2023) introduce a further complication: stated preferences for human vs. algorithmic advice do not reliably predict actual advice use, raising measurement validity concerns for survey-based studies of algorithm aversion.

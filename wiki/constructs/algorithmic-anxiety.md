# Algorithmic Anxiety

Algorithmic anxiety names the psychological distress that emerges when AI systems reshape work, professional identity, and social contracts in ways that feel opaque, uncontrollable, and fundamentally dehumanising. Shekhar and Saurombe (2026) frame it as a distinctive syndrome — not generic technology stress but a response to AI's specific capacity to replace judgment, erode expertise, and render humans superfluous.

## Relation to adjacent constructs

Algorithmic anxiety overlaps with but is distinct from: general technology anxiety (which does not require AI specifically), algorithm aversion (which is a preference or attitude in decision contexts, not a chronic stress state), and technostress (which covers a broader range of technology-related strains). The construct foregrounds *identity* and *dignity* as threatened resources in ways that technostress frameworks do not fully capture.

## Labour discourse implications

In Shekhar and Saurombe (2026), algorithmic anxiety surfaces in online discourse specifically about job displacement. Seven themes from the data illuminate how workers collectively process AI disruption: shattered trust in employers, a sense of corporate betrayal, eroding professional identities, intensifying technostress, devaluation of human expertise, future-orientation anxiety, and cynicism about the possibility of adaptation. These themes suggest that workers frame AI disruption as a violation of implicit psychological contracts rather than as ordinary technological change.

# Cognitive Load

Cognitive Load Theory (CLT), developed by Sweller (1988), distinguishes three types of mental demand imposed on working memory. In AI-education research it provides the primary theoretical language for analysing the tradeoff between AI assistance that reduces frustration and AI assistance that eliminates the productive cognitive effort required for genuine learning.

## Three load types

- **Intrinsic load**: arises from the inherent complexity of the material and the learner's prior knowledge; cannot be reduced by instructional design without altering the task itself.
- **Germane load**: the cognitive effort devoted to schema construction and long-term learning; beneficial and should be preserved.
- **Extraneous load**: imposed by poor instructional design, distracting information, or unnecessary complexity; reduces capacity available for germane processing and should be minimised.

## Relevance to AI-education discourse

The construct is central to a recurring claim in the EdTech literature: that AI tools may reduce extraneous and intrinsic load (improving performance and reducing frustration) while inadvertently also reducing germane load (impairing learning). Bassner et al. (2026) find this pattern empirically — AI conditions showed lower frustration and extraneous load, but no learning advantage over control. Yuxian (2025) finds that combining LLM assistance with critical thinking guidance produces synergistic effects, suggesting germane load can be preserved when scaffolding is designed appropriately.

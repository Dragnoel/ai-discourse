Bearman et al. (2023), on the basis of a critical discourse analysis of 29 articles from the top ten higher education journals (literature to November 2020), argue that the higher education AI research literature constructs AI primarily through two dominant Discourses — imperative change and altering authority — and that social and ethical implications are systematically neglected. The study was published in *Higher Education* (Springer, 2023).

## The claim, in their own framing

Bearman et al. describe their finding as a call for "new research foci that attend to the social implications of AI, including tracing accountability in AI-mediated practices and exploring how AI influences learning and teaching relationships." Their analysis finds that AI is "rarely defined" in the HE literature and that only a "stunningly low" number of papers (2 of 146 in a broader review) consider ethical implications.

## Evidence the proponents offer

- Systematic search of top-10 HE journals for "artificial intelligence" to November 2020; 29 articles met inclusion criteria
- Discourse analysis following Gee (2004): analysed both explicit definitions and implicit associations
- Two Discourses identified through iterative analysis by three researchers; supplementary materials list all 29 included articles
- Cross-referenced against Zawacki-Richter et al. (2019) broader review showing 2/146 articles considered ethical implications

## Counter-positions

No direct counter-claims in this collection. Nikolic et al. (2024) conduct a more recent systematic review with a different analytical framework (UTAUT) and find broader evidence of concern about academic integrity among faculty — suggesting that the discourse may have shifted since Bearman et al.'s 2020 data cutoff. This is not a refutation but a temporal qualification.

## Status
Active. Data cut-off is November 2020; the discourse has evolved substantially since then (ChatGPT was released November 2022). Whether the two Discourses remain dominant in the post-ChatGPT literature is an open empirical question.

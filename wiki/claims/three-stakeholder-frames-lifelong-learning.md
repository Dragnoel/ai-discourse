Eynon and Young (2021), on the basis of 34 in-depth interviews with stakeholders from academia, commerce, and policy, argue that these three groups construct AI for lifelong learning through incompatible technological frames, and that the commercial sector's frame wields disproportionate design influence. The paper was published in *Science, Technology, & Human Values* (SAGE, 2021).

## The claim, in their own framing

Eynon and Young argue that "there are three different technological frames emerging from the three social groups" and call for "greater awareness of the differing technical frames, more interactions among a wider set of relevant social groups, and a stronger focus on the kinds of educational outcomes society seeks" to design AI for learning in ways that "facilitate a democratic education for all."

## Evidence the proponents offer

- 34 in-depth interviews with stakeholders from academia, commerce, and policy
- Document analysis supplementing interview data
- SCOT analytical framework: technological frames derived from iterative analysis
- Three frames identified: academic (AI as methodological tool for understanding learning); commercial (AI as personalisation/delivery mechanism for scalable learning); policy (AI as societal reskilling vehicle for the "AI future")
- Commercial sector identified as most design-influential because they build the systems

## Counter-positions

No direct counter-claims in this collection. Ehsan and Riedl (2024) apply SCOT to a different AI domain (XAI) and reach a pluralism-positive conclusion — interpretive flexibility as productive — which differs from Eynon and Young's concern that commercial frames crowd out alternatives.

## Status
Active. Based on 2021 data; the emergence of ChatGPT in 2022 has substantially altered the landscape of both commercial AI offerings and policy responses.

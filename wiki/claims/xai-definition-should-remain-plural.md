Ehsan and Riedl (2024), in an opinion piece in *Patterns* (CellPress), argue that it is "neither feasible nor desirable" to have a singular monolithic definition of explainable AI (XAI) at the current developmental stage of the field. Drawing on the Social Construction of Technology (SCOT) framework, they argue that XAI's definitional plurality reflects productive interpretive flexibility and that premature closure would stifle innovation.

## The claim, in their own framing

Ehsan and Riedl state: "we argue that it is neither feasible nor desirable to have a singular definition of XAI." They contend that "instead of obsessing over definitional supremacy, it affords progress while simultaneously refining our shared understanding of the concept. It does not mean that we are advocating for epistemological anarchy or definitional relativism."

## Evidence the proponents offer

- Theoretical/analytical argument drawing on SCOT (Bijker 1995; Pinch and Bijker 1987)
- Bicycle analogy: the bicycle evolved from diverse interpretive starting points; premature closure would have foreclosed mountain bikes, electric bikes, and other variants
- Two relevant social groups in XAI identified: NLP and CV; different epistemic cultures and different legitimate interpretations of "explainability"
- Prior work by same authors on Human-Centered XAI (Ehsan & Riedl 2020) provides empirical grounding for the sociotechnical plurality claim

## Counter-positions

No direct counter-claims from this collection. The position is explicitly an argument against standardisation efforts in the XAI community; the counter-position (that a shared definition would accelerate progress) is referenced but not attributed to specific named actors in this paper.

## Status
Active. Opinion piece; empirical tests of whether definitional pluralism aids or hinders XAI progress are not provided in this source.

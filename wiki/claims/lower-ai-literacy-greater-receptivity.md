Tully et al. (2024), across ten studies including four surveys, cross-country data, and six additional experiments, argue that consumers with lower objective AI literacy show greater AI receptivity — contrary to common intuition and existing marketing assumptions. The relationship is mediated by perceptions of AI as magical and by feelings of awe. The paper was published in the *Journal of Marketing* (SAGE/AMA, 2025 volume).

## The claim, in their own framing

Tully et al. describe the "lower literacy-higher receptivity link" as a relationship mediated by AI being perceived as magical: "when AI executes tasks that were once thought to require uniquely human traits, such as feeling emotions, having a personality, and holding beliefs... people with lower AI literacy perceive AI's execution of such tasks as more magical and experience feelings of awe." Higher-literacy consumers recognise that AI does not actually possess human attributes, which reduces perceived magicality and dampens awe.

## Evidence the proponents offer

- Four surveys across different consumer populations (X users, LinkedIn users, business students, Prolific sample); cross-country replication
- Six additional experimental studies varying task type (associated vs. not associated with human attributes) to test moderation
- AI literacy operationalised as objective knowledge (factual test items) rather than self-report
- Mediation confirmed through awe/magicality constructs; alternative explanations (capability perceptions, ethicality, fear of AI's impact on humanity) ruled out

## Counter-positions
- [[Xu and Xiong (2026)]] find that AI literacy (self-reported) positively predicts actual GenAI usage among Chinese FL students — see `wiki/_review.md` for discussion of whether this is a genuine contradiction or a difference in constructs and populations (objective vs. self-report literacy; receptivity vs. actual usage; consumer vs. student)

## Status
Contested with qualification. The contested designation reflects the potential conflict with Xu and Xiong (2026), not a refutation within the same study design. The key empirical question is whether objective and self-reported AI literacy have opposite effects on different outcome variables, which would resolve the apparent contradiction.

Huang et al. (2025), in a mixed-methods study of N=25 undergraduates enrolled in a GenAI-integrated course at Boston University, argue that peer influence and personal ethics (moral obligation) exert greater influence on students' GenAI-assisted cheating behavior than formal institutional policies or class-level deterrents. The paper was published in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* (Elsevier, 2025).

## The claim, in their own framing

Huang et al. find that "peer influence and personal ethics were found to strongly shape cheating behaviors, with class policies enforced by instructors exerting a greater influence on student cheating behavior with GenAI than broader institutional policies." Students perceived the integration of GenAI into education as "inevitable" and showed ambivalence about subtle forms of AI-assisted work, even while clearly opposing overt cheating.

## Evidence the proponents offer

- Mixed-methods: focus groups (N=25) plus individual polls
- Modified TPB framework with moral obligation as fourth construct
- Focus groups captured social and moral reasoning; polls measured individual attitudes
- Peer influence and moral obligation emerged as stronger predictors of cheating behavior than institutional policies
- Students clearly disapproved of overt cheating but showed considerably more ambivalence about subtle uses (e.g., idea generation, brainstorming)

## Counter-positions

No direct counter-claims in this collection. The finding is consistent with the general TPB literature showing that subjective norms and moral obligation predict behavior. The specific claim about institutional policy inefficacy is a practitioner-relevant argument without direct empirical challenge in this corpus.

## Status
Active. Small sample (N=25) at a single US institution; mixed-methods design provides depth but limits generalisability.

Himmelstein and Budescu (2023), in two studies on geopolitical and economic forecasting advice-taking, argue that stated preferences for human versus algorithmic advice do not predict whether or how judges actually use that advice. The paper was published in the *Journal of Behavioral Decision Making* (Wiley, 2023) and was funded by IARPA.

## The claim, in their own framing

Himmelstein and Budescu argue that "verbal statements of preferences and actual use of advice are dissociable constructs" and that much prior algorithm aversion research has conflated them. They find that judges are primarily sensitive to "quantitative features pertaining to the similarity between their initial beliefs and the advice they were offered, such as the distance between them and the relative advisor confidence" — not to the source of the advice.

## Evidence the proponents offer

- Study 1: judges forecasting geopolitical and economic events; domain-specific stated preferences elicited; WOA measured
- Study 2: hybrid condition (human + algorithmic combined) preferred in stated preferences; no difference in WOA versus pure conditions
- Preferences showed domain specificity (human preferred for political, algorithmic for economic forecasting) but preferences did not significantly predict WOA regardless of domain
- Judges primarily updated based on advice-belief distance and advisor confidence, not source identity

## Counter-positions
- [[Logg (2019)]]: WOA was higher for algorithm-labeled advice — but Logg's paradigm controls advice quality, while Himmelstein and Budescu's study uses real forecasting scenarios where quality covaries with source

## Status
Active. Important methodological challenge to studies that measure preference as a proxy for behavior.

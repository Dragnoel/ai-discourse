Logg et al. (2019), across six experiments, argue that lay people assign greater weight to advice when it is labeled as coming from an algorithm than when the same advice is labeled as coming from a human — a phenomenon they term "algorithm appreciation." The paper was published in *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes* (Elsevier, 2019) and directly challenges the prior algorithm aversion literature.

## The claim, in their own framing

Logg et al. frame their finding as a "counter" to the received wisdom that people are skeptical of algorithms. They argue that for many ordinary decisions, "people actually prefer advice from algorithms to advice from people," and that this preference persists when advice appears jointly or separately, and for a range of numeric estimation and forecasting tasks.

## Evidence the proponents offer

- Six experiments; N ranging from approximately 154 to 671+
- JAS paradigm: participants made initial estimate, received advice labeled as algorithmic or human, made final estimate
- Weight on Advice (WOA) as primary DV
- Algorithm-labeled advice produced higher WOA in Experiments 1A (weight estimation), 1B and 1C (song forecasting, attractiveness), and replications
- Effect held in joint and separate presentation conditions (Experiment 2)

## Counter-positions
- [[Himmelstein and Budescu (2023)]]: stated preference for algorithmic advice does not predict actual advice use; the WOA measure may not reflect stated preferences
- [[Jussupow et al. (2024)]]: the "appreciation" effect requires contextualisation within a classification of decision configurations; different study designs may produce different results

## Status
Active. Well-documented effect in quantitative estimation tasks; boundary conditions identified (self-selection, domain expertise, task subjectivity).
